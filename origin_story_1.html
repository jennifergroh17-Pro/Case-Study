Origin Story

We bought a home.
Then the work began.

What started as a cooling issue in a newly purchased home became one of the most complex, multi-domain problems I have ever managed. This is the story of how I got there — and how I chose to respond.

How it unfolded

July 2025 — Closing

A home purchase. A fresh start.

We closed on our home in late July 2025. Like any major purchase, it came with optimism, plans, and the reasonable expectation that what we were buying was what had been represented to us.

Within days, that expectation began to erode.

Phase 1 — First Signal

The house would not cool.

The HVAC system failed to perform. What seemed like a straightforward mechanical issue prompted the first service call — and the first look under the surface of what we had purchased.

That first look raised more questions than it answered.

Process Note

A single system failure is a maintenance issue. The same failure coupled with documentation irregularities is a signal worth investigating. I chose to investigate.

Phase 2 — Escalation

Repair estimates kept coming.

What followed was a steady accumulation of findings. Each inspection surfaced something new. Estimates arrived. Contractors were called. And with every new finding, the picture grew more complex — not simpler.

The issues were not isolated. They were connected. A repair in one area revealed a dependency on another. What had looked like a list was revealing itself to be a system of failures.

Process Note

The instinct at this stage is to fix things as fast as possible. I resisted that instinct. Uncoordinated repairs in a legally complex situation can eliminate leverage, destroy evidence, and create new liability. I slowed down and got structured.

Phase 3 — The Scope Shift

Foundation. Structure. Then the environment.

The turning point came when findings moved beyond mechanical systems into structural and environmental domains. Foundation concerns emerged. Engineering was brought in. And then environmental assessment revealed conditions that changed the nature of the situation entirely — including fungal growth classified at a level requiring professional remediation, and contamination consistent with a biohazard designation.

We were still living in the house.

This was no longer a repair problem. This was a program.

The Reframe

The moment I stopped asking "what needs to be fixed next?" and started asking "what does a controlled response to a systemic failure look like?" — everything changed. Governance came first. Evidence came second. Action came third.

Phase 4 — The Build

Building the framework while inside the problem.

With no external project management support, no dedicated team, and no precedent to follow, I built the response framework from scratch. That meant defining scope, establishing governance controls, engineering an evidence repository, modeling financial scenarios, and managing stakeholders across legal, technical, compliance, and regulatory domains — simultaneously.

The work happened in parallel with daily life. It required sustained discipline, emotional regulation, and the ability to think clearly under conditions of real personal risk.

Process Note

I documented everything in real time. Not because I knew exactly how it would be used, but because I understood that in high-stakes situations, the person with the best records controls the narrative. Documentation became leverage.

Every domain.
At the same time.

By the time the full scope was visible, this situation touched every domain a program manager is trained to handle — and several that require specialized knowledge to navigate. None of them could be addressed in isolation. All of them had to move in parallel.
